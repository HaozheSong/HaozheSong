Driving Fatigue Detection Using Electroencephalogram and Support Vector Machine

Nowadays, self-driving cars are becoming more and more popular in our daily life. One of the functions desired for these cars is to detect driver's fatigue because they are more likely to drowse while the car is in the self-driving mode. To achieve such a function, one practical method is to use machine learning algorithm to analyze the electroencephalogram signal and predict the driving fatigue in advance to prevent accident.

Electroencephalogram

Electroencephalogram, usually abbreviated as EEG, can be used in detecting fatigue while driving because fatigue is highly related with the brain.

As shown in the left picture, EEG raw signals can be sampled from scalp electrodes. The location of the electrodes can be described in the right picture and each electrode is usually called a channel. According to biological research, F, O and P channels are most related to fatigue so EEG signals from these channels are paid more attention in the project.

Through EEGLAB, a MATLAB toolbox specialized in processing EEG signals, we can plot the EEG waves in the time domain and use Fourier Transform to extract Power Spectrum Density(PSD) from a period of the raw signals.

Research links the fatigue with lower frequencies. The larger amplitude of low frequencies is shown in the PSD figures, the more likely is the driver to be tired. Therefore, by analyzing the amplitude of the frequencies, we can make a prediction of the driver's sleepiness.

Support Vector Machine

With PSD as the input features, labels for these PSDs are required to train the SVM. There are many labeling method to evaluate whether the driver is fatigued or not, such as PERCLOS and response time.

I chose response time to label the data. Response time is defined as the time interval between a deviation of the car and the moment the driver starts to response to the deviation. In general, a large amount of the response time often indicates the driver's fatigue because it takes more time for a tired driver to response. So by comparing the driver's response time with a preset threshold, labels for the corresponding feature are obtained.

For example, there are 2 trial events in the figure above. First, a random deviation starts at the moment of the orange line 251. Then the subject of the trial takes some time to respond at the moment of magenta line 253. The interval between 251(or 252) and 253 is defined as response time which is within the research interest.

After training the SVM with the features and labels mentioned above and by optimization, the model achieved a prediction accuracy of 77.8% but more effort is required to improve the Precision rate.
